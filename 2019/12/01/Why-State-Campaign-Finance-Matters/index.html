How Campaign Finance is Reshaping America

Why State Campaign Finance Matters

2019-12-01
Mission

When I meditate on the the state of political contributions, I have a lot of nebulous thoughts and feelings about influence, inequality, and why things in America are the way they are. It can be hard to get grounded, so I tend to look for lessons in specific examples.

I can’t help but turn an eye to Hong Kong’s recent protests. The sheer size of the protests is shocking and far larger than anything I can recall seeing in the US in my lifetime. Ostensibly a tug of war between mainland China’s government and Hong Kong’s semi-autonomous administrative region over extradition treaties, Vox takes a pretty good big-picture look at the current story. However, I’m reminded of an earlier Hong Kong protest. Around 2014 it had been proposed that Hong Kong’s Chief Executive would be popularly elected after being nominated by a “broadly representative nominating committee.”

The description Lawrence Lessig wrote around the time that stuck with me:

This “nominating committee” was to be small, at least relative to the population of Hong Kong. Twelve hundred citizens-about .02 percent of Hong Kong’s population… would have the power to nominate the candidates that the voters would then select among.

Yet those 1,200 were not to be randomly selected. They were instead to be chosen through a complicated procedure that would produce a committee, critics charged, representing a “pro-Beijing, business elite” more than average Hong Kong citizens.

Republic, Lost: Version 2.0

It’s a strikingly unequal sounding system where some get to pick the candidates for whom everyone else will vote. Hong Kong implements similar layered voting measures for its Legislative Council as well, which may help to explain the disconnect between the demands of the voters and the objectives of their politicians in 2019. Lessig labelled it Tweedism, after Boss Tweed from New York’s Tamany Hall era. Tweed’s own words on the matter give the game away – I don’t care who does the electing, as long as I get to do the nominating.

It’s not hard to draw a parallel between political nominating committee and supposed elite donors. Among other things, The United States was designed to be a republic wholly dependent upon its voters. Any structures, legal or otherwise, that create a different dependence seemingly damage political equality.

More insidiously, big donation power provides access. Paul Krugman wrote an opinion piece on Excessive Wealth Disorder that talks more about the power of money to set an agenda. I think the entire thing is worth a read, but he specifically distinguishes between campaign contributions (item 3 on his list of influence points) and agenda-setting:

4. Defining the agenda: Through a variety of channels — media ownership, think tanks, and the simple tendency to assume that being rich also means being wise — the 0.1 percent has an extraordinary ability to set the agenda for policy discussion, in ways that can be sharply at odds with both a reasonable assessment of priorities and public opinion more generally.

Notes on Excessive Wealth Disorder

I’m inclined to agree with the definition, but don’t see these two items as necessarily separate. The power to produce big donations creates a political dependence that often means getting a seat at the table and potentially setting an agenda completely contrary to the wants and needs of the typical voter.

Lessig, Krugman and others often put their emphasis on the federal level, but state-level action serves as a kind of foundation for that stage. State government is the proving ground for future Congress people, Senators and Presidents, a testing lab for model legislation, a constant pathway for suits that go to The Supreme Court and the place where congressional district lines are drawn. If Tweedism exists, it begins here.

And there are, potentially, so many more problems than just Tweedism. MapChange’s mission begins here – working to explore state campaign finance systems and better understand how they shape the relationship between voters and political outcomes.

About
